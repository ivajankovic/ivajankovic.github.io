Learning Algorithms for Discovering Synthetic Lethal Metabolic Interventions

Doctoral research framework integrating high-dimensional sampling, control-theoretic viability, game theory, and biological validation.

I. Computational Geometry

Boundary sampling of convex polytopes

  • Implementation of Billiard Walk and Shake-and-Bake Walk algorithms.
  • Sampling boundary facets of feasible regions.
  • Detecting active constraints and collapse directions.
  • Supporting geometry-based vulnerability discovery.

Geometric measures of biological robustness

  • Evaluating volume, anisotropy, and directional widths.
  • Analyzing boundary activity and active constraints.
  • Quantifying metabolic flexibility versus fragility beyond standard FBA/FVA pointwise analysis.

Dynamic volume updating via random-walk sampling

  • Managing sequential constraint additions (P₀ ⊃ P₁ ⊃ ... ⊃ Pₜ).
  • Optimizing sample reuse and applying annealed cuts.
  • Formulating a scalable reward proxy for reinforcement learning frameworks.

II. Biocontrol & Dynamical Systems

Dynamical viability filtering for GRNs

  • Evaluating signed GRN topology (activation / repression).
  • Computing gauge-cooperativity as a graph-computable certificate.
  • Analyzing compact attractors under biological boundedness and polyhedral trapping blocks for attractor localisation.
  • Assessing robustness margins under kinetic uncertainty.

Topology refinement

  • Framing failed GRNs as topology-editing problems.
  • Executing minimal edge deletion or sign-flip operations.
  • Utilizing distance-to-viability scores to preserve high-confidence biological edges.

Proof-of-concept validation

  • Testing on canonical GRN motifs (e.g., mutual repression toggle as a certified case).
  • Analyzing incoherent feed-forward loops as failed cases and the repressilator as an oscillatory non-certified case.
  • Simulating perturbations and comparing overall robustness.

III. Game Theory & Reinforcement Learning

Game-theoretic intervention design

  • Modeling interventions as players/actions within tumour versus healthy feasible spaces.
  • Defining utility functions (Tumour collapse - Healthy damage - Cost).
  • Evaluating potential games for stable multi-target strategies and establishing static baselines for intervention selection.

Reinforcement learning for sequential discovery

  • Defining environment states as current constrained polytopes.
  • Defining actions as gene/reaction constraint additions.
  • Assigning rewards based on selective robustness loss.
  • Training policies to learn ordered intervention combinations.

IV. Biomedical Impact & Experimental Validation

Cancer-specific models

  • Reconstructing tumour and healthy-cell GEMs.
  • Comparing existing reconstruction methods.
  • Testing whether better models improve geometry-based discovery.

Experimental validation

  • Prioritising selective synthetic lethal candidates.
  • Validating with DepMap and gene essentiality data.
  • Undertaking experimental missions in collaboration with biological research groups.
